Fine-Tuning Your First Model

December 28, 2025 · 10 min read

Fine-tuning transforms a general-purpose language model into a specialist. Whether you want a model that writes like you, understands your company's jargon, or follows a specific output format, fine-tuning is the most reliable path. Here's exactly how I do it.

1. Choose Your Base Model

I recommend starting with Mistral 7B or Llama 3 8B. They're small enough to train on a single GPU, large enough to be capable, and permissively licensed for commercial use. Bigger isn't always better — a well-tuned 7B model often outperforms a raw 70B on narrow tasks.

2. Prepare Your Dataset

Quality beats quantity. I've had excellent results with just 500 carefully curated examples. Each example needs:

  • Instruction: What you want the model to do
  • Input (optional): Context or data to process
  • Output: The ideal response

Clean your data aggressively. Remove duplicates, fix formatting inconsistencies, and verify that outputs are actually correct. Garbage in, garbage out applies doubly to fine-tuning.

3. Set Up the Training Environment

I use Unsloth with QLoRA for memory-efficient training. The setup looks like this:

from unsloth import FastLanguageModel
import torch

model, tokenizer = FastLanguageModel.from_pretrained(
    model_name = "unsloth/mistral-7b-v0.3",
    max_seq_length = 2048,
    dtype = torch.bfloat16,
    load_in_4bit = True,
)

4. Train with Care

Use a low learning rate (1e-4 to 2e-4) and train for 1-3 epochs. Monitor validation loss — if it starts rising while training loss keeps falling, you're overfitting. Early stopping is your friend.

5. Evaluate Honestly

Test on held-out examples that weren't in your training set. Better yet, have someone else evaluate the outputs without knowing which is the fine-tuned model. Human judgment matters more than perplexity scores.

"A fine-tuned model is a reflection of your data. If your data has biases, gaps, or errors, your model will amplify them."
