Prompt Engineering Deep Dive

December 15, 2025 · 8 min read

"Act as an expert" is the console.log of prompt engineering — it works, but it's just the beginning. After thousands of production prompts, I've developed patterns that dramatically improve reliability, consistency, and output quality. Let's go deep.

Structure is Everything

LLMs are pattern-matching engines. The more structure you give them, the better they perform. I use a consistent template:

  • Role: Who the model should be
  • Context: Background information it needs
  • Task: Exactly what to do
  • Format: How the output should look
  • Constraints: What to avoid or include

This isn't just for clarity — it gives the model multiple anchors to latch onto, reducing ambiguity.

Chain-of-Thought Actually Works

Adding "Think step by step" to complex reasoning tasks can improve accuracy by 40% or more. But don't stop there. Force the model to show its work before giving the answer. This catches errors in reasoning that would otherwise be hidden.

For math and logic, I use: "First, outline your reasoning. Then, provide the final answer." The intermediate steps let me validate the logic and debug when things go wrong.

Temperature: A Practical Guide

  • 0.0 – 0.3: Classification, extraction, structured output. Predictable, repeatable.
  • 0.4 – 0.7: General conversation, brainstorming, creative writing. Balanced variety.
  • 0.8 – 1.2: Poetry, wild ideas, exploring edge cases. High creativity, lower reliability.

I almost never go above 0.7 for production. The novelty isn't worth the inconsistency.

Few-Shot Examples Beat Instructions

A single well-crafted example teaches more than a paragraph of instructions. For formatting tasks, I provide 2-3 input/output pairs in the prompt. The model infers the pattern and applies it more reliably than if I tried to describe the rules explicitly.

"The prompt is the interface. A well-designed prompt is as important as a well-designed API."
