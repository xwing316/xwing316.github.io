Best Dev Tools of the Year

December 20, 2025 · 6 min read

Every year I audit my tooling stack. This year's theme was speed without clutter — tools that get out of the way and let me think. Here are the standouts that earned permanent spots in my workflow.

Editor: Cursor

I was skeptical of AI-assisted editors until I tried Cursor. It's not about writing code for you — it's about removing friction. Inline diffs, natural language refactoring, and intelligent autocomplete that actually understands your codebase. I still write most logic myself, but the boilerplate and repetitive edits are handled gracefully.

Terminal: Ghostty + Zellij

Ghostty is fast. Ludicrously fast. GPU-accelerated rendering means scrolling through 100K lines of logs is instant. Paired with Zellij for terminal multiplexing, I have a persistent workspace that survives SSH disconnects and organizes my panes logically. No more tmux config rabbit holes.

CLI Power Tools

  • ripgrep + fd: Searching and finding files at speeds that make find and grep feel prehistoric.
  • fzf: Fuzzy everything — history, files, git branches. I use it hundreds of times a day without thinking.
  • jq + yq: JSON and YAML manipulation from the command line. Essential for pipeline debugging.
  • bat: Syntax-highlighted file previews with git integration. Replaces cat entirely.

Productivity

Raycast replaced Spotlight, Alfred, and three separate apps. Extensions for everything from window management to clipboard history to GitHub issue search — all in a clean, keyboard-driven interface.

For note-taking, I moved everything to Obsidian. The local-first approach means my notes are plain Markdown files I can grep, version control, or migrate anywhere. The graph view is a nice bonus, but the real win is knowing my data is mine.

"The best tool is the one you don't notice. If you're fighting your setup, you're not shipping."
